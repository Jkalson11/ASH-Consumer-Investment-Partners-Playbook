Fund Strategy

Fund I will make 8–12 concentrated investments in founder-led consumer businesses across the Southeast, deploying the ASH operating system to drive EBITDA expansion, unit economics improvement, and scalable growth. We target businesses with $2M–$15M of EBITDA where our operational involvement is the primary value driver — not financial engineering.

Prior Investment Structure

Prior to Fund I, ASH Consumer Investment Partners deployed capital through individual Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) on a deal-by-deal basis, building a portfolio of consumer businesses including The Chastain, Perrine's Wine Shop, and Jackson Fine Art. Fund I represents the institutionalization of this approach — bringing the same operating discipline and deal selectivity to a formal fund structure with LP governance, reporting, and alignment.

What Makes Fund I Different

  • Operator-led, not financial engineering — Value is created through the ASH operating system, not leverage or multiple arbitrage
  • Concentrated and high-conviction — Fewer, larger bets in businesses where ASH has deep sector knowledge
  • Southeast-first advantage — Proprietary deal flow from 20+ years of regional relationships, before businesses reach competitive auction processes
  • Institutional infrastructure on a mid-market platform — Scorecard discipline, governance rigor, and management cadence that larger funds apply, deployed at the $5M–$30M equity check size where competition is thinner

Liquidity

Exit Strategy

ASH Consumer Investment Partners targets a 5–7 year hold per investment. We build businesses to be attractive to multiple buyer types, and we plan exit pathways from the moment of investment — not at the end of the hold period. Diversified exit paths reduce reliance on any single buyer market.

Primary Path

Strategic Sale

Sale to a strategic acquirer — a larger consumer company, restaurant group, retail platform, or brand operator — seeking to acquire a profitable, well-run business in the Southeast with a proven operating infrastructure. Our operating system makes businesses more attractive acquisition targets by professionalizing reporting, reducing key-person risk, and demonstrating EBITDA predictability.

Secondary Path

Management Buyout / Recap

Recapitalization or partial buyout by management, enabling the operator to take back control of a now-institutionalized business. This is particularly relevant in founder-led situations where succession and continuity are core to the investment thesis. ASH takes its return through the recap while management retains or grows their ownership stake.

Tertiary Path

PE Sponsor Sale

Sale to a larger private equity sponsor seeking to deploy capital into a proven, scaled consumer platform in the Southeast. As Southeast consumer businesses professionalize, they attract growing interest from mid-market and large-cap PE firms who lack the regional relationships and operating infrastructure to source and develop these assets themselves.

Return Construction

  • EBITDA growth — Primary return driver; 3–5× EBITDA expansion through operating system application
  • Multiple expansion — Businesses that enter at 4–6× EBITDA exit at 7–10× as they institutionalize
  • Modest leverage — Used selectively and conservatively; never as a primary return driver
  • Target: 2–3× MOIC / 20%+ net IRR across the portfolio over a 5–7 year hold
Risk Disclosure

Risk Factors

Investing in private equity involves significant risk, including the potential loss of invested capital. Prospective investors should carefully consider the following risk factors before making an investment decision. This is not an exhaustive list — prospective LPs should review all offering documents carefully and consult their own legal, financial, and tax advisors.

Investment Risk
Illiquidity & Long Hold Periods

Investments in Fund I are illiquid and subject to a 5–7 year hold per position, with a full fund life of approximately 10 years. Investors should not expect to redeem capital or receive distributions on a predictable schedule. There is no secondary market for LP interests.

Market Risk
Consumer Sector Cyclicality

Consumer businesses are subject to macroeconomic cycles, discretionary spending shifts, and changes in consumer preferences. An economic downturn, recession, or significant shift in consumer behavior could adversely affect portfolio company revenues and valuations.

Operational Risk
Execution & Operating Improvement

Fund I's return thesis depends substantially on ASH's ability to implement operational improvements across portfolio companies. There is no guarantee that the operating system will produce the anticipated results in every investment, and outcomes will vary by business.

Concentration Risk
Geographic & Sector Concentration

Fund I is concentrated in the Southeast U.S. and within the consumer sector. Regional economic conditions, natural disasters, regulatory changes, or sector-specific headwinds could disproportionately affect the portfolio relative to a more diversified fund.

Exit Risk
Realization Uncertainty

The ability to exit investments at target valuations depends on M&A market conditions, strategic buyer appetite, credit market availability, and business performance at the time of exit. There is no guarantee that exit timelines or valuations will meet projections.

Key Person Risk
Management & Team Dependency

Fund I's performance is dependent on the continued involvement of ASH's senior team and the operator-partners at each portfolio company. The loss of key personnel at either the fund manager or portfolio company level could adversely affect investment outcomes.

05 — Operating Motions

Value-Creation Operating Motions

ASH deploys one of two operating motions based on the business's starting point—ensuring our approach is stage-appropriate, measurable, and aligned to value creation.

Motion 1

Growth & Scale

For growth-stage brands: institutionalize the operating infrastructure required to scale without margin erosion or brand degradation. We focus on repeatable systems, leadership capacity, and durable unit economics.

Characteristics

  • Core return driver: revenue growth, market expansion
  • Primary value creation: scaling what already works
  • Upside: largely through revenue growth and slight margin improvement
  • Capital intensity: often requires follow-on capital

Primary Focus Areas

  • Scalable org design and leadership capacity
  • Throughput and capacity planning
  • Durable unit economics at scale
  • Operating cadence and management systems
  • Repeatability (SOPs, training, brand standards)
Motion 2

Performance Optimization

For established assets: improve predictability, profitability, and cash generation through execution rigor and structural efficiency. We prioritize near-term EBITDA improvement while building the cadence to sustain gains.

Characteristics

  • Core return driver: multiple expansion, margin expansion
  • Primary value creation: fixing underperformance
  • Upside: largely through key operational lever pulls
  • Capital intensity: often requires patient capital

Primary Focus Areas

  • Margin expansion (COGS, labor, waste, pricing/mix)
  • Cash and working capital discipline
  • Execution consistency and manager routines
  • Performance management system
  • Targeted growth where ROI is clear

Sub-Vertical Translation

  • Products — demand + channel economics + margin by channel + inventory turns
  • Services — utilization/capacity + CAC/LTV + labor delivery model + retention
  • Hospitality — throughput + labor scheduling discipline + mix/pricing + COGS and waste controls
06 — Financial Anchor

Financial North Star: Adjusted EBITDA

ASH anchors performance management on Adjusted EBITDA and the operational drivers that expand it: gross margin, labor/delivery efficiency, fixed-cost leverage, and disciplined discretionary spend.

Where appropriate, we track contribution margin by channel, site, or service line as a diagnostic layer to understand why EBITDA is moving.

Adjusted EBITDA Conventions

  • Establish definitions at the start — owner comp, one-time items, startup/expansion costs, extraordinary items
  • Maintain a consistent reconciliation so operators and investors read the same scorecard
  • Treat adjustments as transparency tools — not hiding places
07 — Diagnostic Framework

The 14-Day Diagnostic

At the start of each partnership—and at major inflection points—ASH runs a structured 14-day diagnostic to identify the primary constraint and route the engagement into the appropriate motion.

Outputs by Day 14

  • Motion selection (Scale vs Optimize) and sequencing
  • Top 5 priorities with owners, timelines, and KPI targets
  • Baseline scorecard with agreed definitions
  • Risk and dependency log (keyperson, vendor, cash, operational risk)

Diagnostic Lenses

A Financial Performance & EBITDA Quality
B Revenue Engine (Demand & Mix)
C Unit Economics
D Capacity, Throughput & Delivery
E Cash & Working Capital
F Org, Talent & Accountability
G Systems, Controls & Data Integrity

Routing Logic

→

If the constraint is capacity, infrastructure, leadership depth, or repeatability → route to Growth & Scale

→

If the constraint is economics leakage, execution inconsistency, or weak controls/cash conversion → route to Performance Optimization

→

If both are true → stabilize economics and controls first, then scale
